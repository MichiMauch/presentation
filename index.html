But I don’t think we need to go that far today. After all, we enjoy working with our customers as equals. Still, there is a small grain of truth in that statement.

It’s usually not that difficult to keep our customers happy. I have a little story from one of my first jobs that illustrates this.

Back in around the year 2000, I worked as a service technician, repairing photocopiers and printers in large office spaces. At that time, it was common for 50 people in an open-plan office to share a single printer or copier. And if that machine stopped working, they couldn’t do their jobs properly.

So, I would arrive at the office, and the manager—already quite nervous— would tell me that his team couldn’t print or copy, and that it was absolutely critical to get the machine running again. Then came my favorite question: “What do you think? How long will it take to fix the machine?”

As someone with experience, my response was always: “Well, it looks complicated… I’d say maybe two hours, or if things go well, maybe just 90 minutes.” This, of course, knowing that the machine would most likely be up and running in just 30 minutes.

The manager’s response: “Oh yes, that would be great. Thank you so much!” He was already satisfied because he had received a clear and concrete answer.

Then, when I actually finished after 45 minutes, he was even happier and even gave me a 20-franc tip. On top of that, he could take credit for organizing everything so efficiently. A classic win-win situation!

Service quality is the dominant competitive factor

What I want to highlight is how important good customer support is.

I would even say that good customer support is more important than good code… please don’t be mad at me.

I want to show you some impressive numbers.

88%

Service quality as a purchasing criterion: Studies by Armonia Deutschland GmbH show that 88% of German consumers consider service quality to be a decisive purchasing criterion – price competition ranks secondary.

12% / 63%

Customer satisfaction vs. price: A long-term analysis of retail banking customers shows that only 12% of customers leave the service provider because the prices are high, but 63% of customers leave the service provider because they are not satisfied with the service.

37%

Service as an innovation accelerator: Customers don't just rely on innovation, but above all on good service. Data from electronics manufacturers show that products with strong support are accepted 37% faster than technologically better alternatives. The reason? Good service provides security and lowers the barrier to new technologies - more than any feature.

20/60/20 Model: The 20/60/20 distribution is a simplified model often used in customer value analysis to categorize groups based on their customer lifetime value (CLV). It is similar to the Pareto Principle (80/20 rule), which states that a small portion of customers (often 20%) bring the most value to a company.

How to Maximize Value from Customer Support?

📌 Understand Your Customers

📌 Proactive Communication

📌 Speed & Accessibility

📌 Personalization & Empathy

📌 Continuous Improvement

Introduction: “Now that we understand why support is crucial, the next question is: How can we extract the most value from it? Here are five key strategies.”

1. Understand Your Customers: - “Without understanding customer pain points, we can't deliver great support.” - “Regular surveys, customer feedback, and analyzing support tickets help identify patterns.”

2. Proactive Communication: - “Why wait for customers to complain? We should anticipate and resolve issues before they escalate.” - “Example: Automated status updates for support tickets or FAQs that answer common questions in advance.”

3. Speed & Accessibility: - “Fast support is great support. Long wait times lead to frustration.” - “A multichannel approach: Not every customer wants to call—offer email, chatbots, and help desks.”

4. Personalization & Empathy: - “No one wants to feel like just another ticket number. Personalized responses build customer loyalty.” - “Support agents should use the customer’s name and avoid copy-paste responses.”

5. Continuous Improvement: - “Support is a process that must constantly evolve.” - “Support ticket data, recurring issues, and customer feedback should be used to refine products and processes over time.”

Call-to-Action: “These five strategies aren’t just theory—they are tested and turn customer support into a real competitive advantage!”

The "Golden Rule" of Support

"Make life easier for customers, not more complicated!"

Make life easier for customers, not more harder!

Introduction: “There’s one simple rule in customer support: Make life easier for customers, not harder. This is the Golden Rule of Support.”

Explain the Quote: - "Customers don't want obstacles, they want solutions." - "The goal is to reduce friction and help them get what they need as quickly and easily as possible."

🛠 Tools & Processes:

✅ AI-powered support for fast responses

✅ Self-service portals for recurring questions

✅ Clear and easy-to-understand guides

✅ Well-trained support teams

How do we achieve this?

  • AI-powered support: "Fast responses, automated help, and intelligent suggestions make support more efficient."
  • Self-service portals: "Let customers find answers on their own for common questions."
  • Clear and easy-to-understand guides: "No one likes confusing manuals. Keep instructions simple and actionable."
  • Well-trained support teams: "A great support team makes all the difference—investing in training pays off."

“If we follow this Golden Rule and make things easier for customers, they will reward us with loyalty.”

Measuring Support Success

"Which metrics show that our support truly delivers value?"

Introduction: “Support success is not just about ‘feeling good’—we need to measure it. Here are four key metrics that tell us if our support is truly effective.”

📊 Metrics:

✅ First Response Time (FRT)

✅ Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT)

✅ Retention Rate

✅ Customer Effort Score (CES)

1. First Response Time (FRT): - "Customers expect quick responses. A long response time increases frustration and churn." - "The faster we respond, the higher the percived service quality."

2. Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT): - "Direct feedback from customers about their support experience." - "Simple post-interaction surveys help us track satisfaction trends."

3. Retention Rate: - "Great support strengthens loyalty." - "Do customers stay after a support case, or do they leave? Tracking retention tells us how well we are doing."

4. Customer Effort Score (CES): - "How much effort did the customer need to put in to solve their issue?" - "The lower the effort, the better the experience—and the higher the chance of future engagement."

Call-to-Action: “If we improve these four key metrics, our support transforms from a cost center into a real value driver for our company.”

🎯 Well-Trained Support Teams

"A great support team makes all the difference—investing in training pays off."

Introduction: “One of the most overlooked but critical aspects of great customer support is training. No matter how advanced our tools are, a well-trained team makes all the difference.”

Why does training matter? - "Support teams are the direct connection to customers—how they handle interactions defines the company’s reputation." - "Untrained or poorly trained staff lead to inconsistent experiences, frustration, and even lost customers."

Experience matters—why we shouldn’t put interns or juniors on the front line: - "The first support interaction can define whether a customer stays or leaves. It’s not the place for trial and error." - "Experienced support agents can quickly identify problems, provide solutions, and de-escalate situations—skills that come with practice." - "Junior staff can and should learn, but their role should be in the background first, gaining experience before taking on direct customer interactions." - "Customers expect expertise. If they feel they’re talking to someone inexperienced, they lose confidence in the company." - "A well-trained senior support team is an investment in customer satisfaction and loyalty."

Call-to-Action: “If we want support to be a strategic asset, we must prioritize experience and training—because great customer service is not an entry-level job.”
